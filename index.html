Bitcoin

Bitcoin (BTC) represents a new paradigm in public, decentralised digital payments infrastructure, open to all. Anyone, anywhere, can send or receive BTC without relying on banks or intermediaries. It operates on a peer-to-peer network secured by cryptography (SHA-256) and recorded on a transparent ledger called the blockchain. Transactions are verified by “miners” who use computing power to repeatedly apply SHA-256 to block data until they find a hash that meets the difficulty target of the network — a process known as proof-of-work (PoW). This system ensures that all transactions are valid and secure.

The Core Value Proposition

BTC has no intrinsic value but derives its worth from the utility of its decentralised network and the collective belief in its future importance.

Original Purpose

BTC was introduced as a “peer-to-peer electronic cash system” designed to enable fast, low-cost, borderless transactions without relying on trusted third parties.

Evolution

Over time BTC has moved along the “moneyness” spectrum — shifting from primarily functioning as cash to being regarded mainly as a store of value. Owing to relatively slow settlement times, occasional spikes in transaction fees during network congestion and price volatility; however, it could shift again as the network matures.

Environmental Impact Assessment

Energy Consumption Reality

  • Scale: The BTC network currently consumes an estimated 100–175 terawatt-hours (TWh), comparable to entire countries. In the long term, as adoption plateaus, energy consumption is expected to stabilise as block rewards diminish and the network fully matures. - For comparison, the global banking system consumes about 238 TWh/year and gold mining about 240 TWh/year.
  • Geography: Mining concentrates in regions with cheap electricity, from renewable-rich to coal-dependent areas.
  • Efficiency: Modern ASIC miners are more efficient than early hardware, but total network consumption continues to grow.
  • E-Waste Challenge: Specialised mining hardware becomes obsolete quickly, creating electronic waste streams.
  • Grid Impact: Environmental impact heavily depends on local energy mix where mining occurs.

Sustainability Considerations

The energy use of BTC is substantial and its environmental footprint—including electronic waste—remains a real concern with only an estimated 52% of mining powered by renewables. That being said the industry is increasingly utilising waste energy and exploring more sustainable practices, but ultimately still guided by economic incentives. Again, these externalities should be considered in the broader context of sectors, like banking, data centres and gold mining, all of which also have significant but often overlooked environmental impacts.

My risk assessment

Infrastructure Limitations

  • Scalability Limitations: BTC’s transaction throughput is limited (~7 transactions ps), which may hinder mass adoption without effective l-2 solutions or protocol upgrades.

    - However, the Lightning Network is showing meaningful progress. As of 2025 it supports over 5,500 BTC in public capacity, with thousands of active nodes and real-world usage.

  • Layer-2 Dependency Risks Lightning and other layer-2 solutions rely on the base layer security, but also introduce new centralisation and liquidity risks (routing hubs or channel management). A failure in major Lightning hubs could harm confidence.
  • Energy Intensity: POW consensus requires enormous energy consumption.
  • Centralisation of Mining Geography A large portion of BTC hash power is often concentrated in a few countries, making the network vulnerable to regional regulation, political instability, or coordinated crackdowns.
  • Technological Obsolescence: Superior innovations like sharding which are more efficient could erode dominance.
  • Network Attack Vectors: Risks such as 51% attacks, eclipse attacks, or vulnerabilities in wallet software remain possible, especially if mining power consolidates.
  • Economic Incentives: As block rewards halve, revenue will increasingly depend on transaction fees, affecting network security and fee dynamics unpredictably.

    - The Bank for International Settlements estimates that, to maintain current security without subsidies, average fees might need to exceed US$100 per transaction – an impractical level for most users.

  • Strategic Accumulation: Large actors, could amass BTC to exert market influence or destabilise confidence, though such control is costly and difficult.

    - An NBER study found that the top 10,000 Bitcoin investors control roughly one-third of the total supply.

  • Quantum Computing Threats: SHA-256 mining is largely resistant to quantum speed-ups, but its ECDSA signatures could eventually be broken by a sufficiently advanced quantum computer, enabling theft from addresses with exposed public keys. The principal threat is not the technical upgrade itself, but achieving network-wide consensus in time. A hostile government or large player could attempt to delay or block consensus through miner control, exchange pressure, or disinformation. The highest-risk scenario is a covert quantum attack exploited before detection; in such circumstances, the vulnerable chain would be rapidly drained, forcing a chaotic migration to the secure chain.

Value & Adoption

  • Lack of Intrinsic Value: Often cited as a weakness of BTC, since it is not tied to cash flow or industrial use. Supporters argue that the value lies in the network itself — its security, global reach, and censorship resistance.
  • Regulatory Uncertainty: Governments might impose restrictive regulations or bans, threatening adoption and price stability. JMPC flags as a major concern.
  • Adoption Barriers: Despite growing adoption, BTC still faces challenges around usability, custody, and education that may slow mainstream acceptance.
  • Liquidity Risks: High price volatility can undermine BTC’s role as a stable store of value or medium of exchange in the short term.
  • On-chain Privacy Limitations The ledger is fully public, meaning transactions are permanently traceable. Chain analysis can deanonymise users, and privacy upgrades face political and technical resistance.
  • Custodial Risk: Many BTC users rely on centralised exchanges or custodial wallets. These can be hacked, mismanaged, or seized by authorities, undermining the “self-sovereign” value proposition.
  • Artificially Enhanced Misinformation: The proliferation of AI-generated content and deepfakes could exacerbate misinformation around BTC undermining public trust.

The Satoshi Nakamoto Enigma

  • The true identity of the BTC creator Satoshi Nakamoto remains unknown — a potential black swan risk. Blockchain analysis (the Patoshi pattern) suggests Satoshi may have mined around 4-5% of the total maximum supply of BTC in its first year, though it remains unproven without movement or cryptographic signatures. If even a small portion of these coins, believed to be untouched were ever moved or sold, it could shake market confidence and have unpredictable consequences for the legitimacy of BTC, regulatory treatment, and public trust. As the architect of a tool designed to operate outside state monetary systems, anonymity fuels both mythology and lingering uncertainty about who could ultimately influence the narrative.

Tethergate

  • Some analysts warn that large issuances of the stablecoin Tether (USDT) may have artificially inflated the price of BTC during key market cycles. Although no full independent audit has ever been conducted, the settlement with the New York Attorney General confirmed that Tether had misrepresented its reserves—at one stage, it was only 74 % backed by fiat. This lends credence to concerns that part of BTC’s historic price growth may have been driven by unbacked capital flows, posing systemic risks if confidence in USDT were to falter.

Strategy

  • Strategy transformed itself from a conventional software company into one of the largest corporate holders of BTC, often using debt to increase its exposure. Analysts warn that this leveraged approach amplifies volatility for both shares and the wider BTC market, especially if falling prices force large-scale liquidations. Notably the firm has a past filled with controversies: during the dot-com bubble, their share price collapsed after an SEC investigation and a major accounting restatement. These factors have led some to view Strategy as a potential point of instability within the BTC ecosystem.

Belief Defiance

  • Many BTC supporters react defensively when its merits are questioned, often relying on slogans or mainstream talking points rather than evidence. This herd-like reinforcement makes reasoned debate difficult and can shut down critical analysis.

On the positive side

  • Absolute Digital Scarcity: BTC has a fixed and transparent supply, unlike physical commodities that could be expanded through mining innovations, like eventual space mining.
  • Network Effects: First-mover advantage, a large global user base, deep liquidity, and the most battle-tested blockchain security give BTC a resilience that is hard for competitors to match.
  • Store-of-Value Narrative: Increasingly seen as a hedge against fiat inflation and geopolitical risks.
  • Libertarian Ideals: Rooted in decentralisation and censorship resistance, it continues to attract ideological and financial support.
  • Portability of Wealth: BTC can be stored in a hardware wallet, memorised as a seed phrase, or transmitted digitally — allowing large amounts of value to cross borders invisibly.
  • Immutability: Once confirmed, transactions cannot be altered or reversed, providing a high level of settlement finality that is rare in the digital realm.
  • Born from Distrust: Created in the wake of the financial crisis, Bitcoin was designed to operate without reliance on banks, governments, or any central authority — a principle underscored by the Genesis Block message referencing bank bailouts, signalling resistance to rule changes by powerful interests.

Some critics liken BTC to a decentralised Ponzi scheme, noting that early adopters have disproportionately benefited as new participants enter the market and drive demand. However, unlike a true Ponzi, BTC has no central operator, no guaranteed returns and operates in an open, transparent market. While its price is influenced by continual demand and speculative interest, it also offers real-world utility as a censorship-resistant, transferable digital asset with a fixed supply — factors that set it apart from fraudulent investment schemes.

Like many transformative innovations, BTC could either revolutionise finance or remain a speculative bubble akin to historic manias.

My stance earlier was intentionally cautious, because one should be with regards to any speculative asset. The world of cryptocurrencies is notoriously deceptive and when exchanges place marketing at the core of their business model, it raises red flags.

That being said many observers argue that fiat currency systems are in a late-cycle phase marked by rising debt loads, persistent deficits, financial repression, and faster debasement of purchasing power. There is a visible shift back toward scarce, harder assets. Consequently, maybe the paradigm of BTC is now the answer.

Even if BTC fails due to its own flaws, the pressures of rising authoritarianism — from tighter capital controls to deeper surveillance and monetary debasement — may sustain the demand for systems with similar properties. Even with flawed execution, the concept of a decentralised and censorship resistant asset could inspire future alternatives.

In either a fractured or a globalised world, abundant energy from nuclear breakthroughs and a successful BTC transition to quantum-resistant cryptography could converge to position it as the ultimate store of value for global wealth preservation.
I personally remain unconvinced, particularly as the market is still in its infancy and the asset remains highly speculative. Nevertheless, the potential upside is so substantial that a hedge — using a DCA approach — seems sensible.
